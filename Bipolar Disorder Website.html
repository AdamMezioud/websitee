<> Bipolar disorder, previously known as manic depression, is a mental disorder characterized by periods of depression and periods of abnormally elevated mood that each last from days to weeks If the elevated mood is severe or associated with psychosis, it is called mania; if it is less severe, it is called hypomania During mania, an individual behaves or feels abnormally energetic, happy or irritable, and they often make impulsive decisions with little regard for the consequences. There is usually also a reduced need for sleep during manic phases During periods of depression, the individual may experience crying and have a negative outlook on life and poor eye contact with others The risk of suicide is high; over a period of 20 years, 6% of those with bipolar disorder died by suicide, while 30\40% engaged in self-harm. Other mental health issues, such as anxiety disorders and substance use disorders, are commonly associated with bipolar disorder.

In bipolar 1, the highs are called mania. These episodes are more severe than bipolar 2. For example, while a person with bipolar 2 may feel they need less sleep than usual during a manic episode, an individual with bipolar 1 may go for days without sleeping at all. Symptoms of mania often lead to disruption in daily life and social activities, and can cause problems in personal relationships. While there's no surefire way to completely prevent bipolar disorder, there are several strategies that may contribute to maintaining mental well-being and reducing the risk of experiencing the disorder. Engaging in a healthy lifestyle, practicing self-care, and seeking professional help when needed can all play a role in supporting mental health. Remember that individual experiences and risk factors can vary, so it's essential to consult with medical professionals for personalized guidance. Engaging in proactive steps to promote mental well-being can be a key approach in potentially reducing the risk of bipolar disorder. Maintaining a balanced and healthy lifestyle through regular exercise, proper nutrition, and sufficient sleep can contribute to emotional stability. Managing stress through relaxation techniques, mindfulness, and engaging in hobbies or activities that bring joy can aid in emotional resilience. Developing strong social connections and a support network can provide an outlet for sharing feelings and seeking guidance.
